How is the diagnosis of the automatic transmission (AT) performed according to all the rules?

Modern automatic transmissions are very “smart” and reliable mechanisms. Car owners know that driving and owning a car with an AT on board is a pleasure. The unit does not require adjustments or settings. It is enough to perform timely maintenance, change the oil to high-quality oil, and you can expect a long lifespan from the transmission. But this is only possible under ideal conditions, following all operational requirements, timely maintenance, and driving on smooth and quality roads. Unfortunately, it is rare to find such conditions. Alas, a combination of negative factors leads to premature wear of parts, and repair is required. As a preventive measure or to save on repair costs, it is recommended to perform an AT diagnosis. This is one of the most important stages of repairing a variator or automatic transmission. We will discuss this procedure in detail in this article.

How the AT works

To understand the principle and necessity of conducting diagnostic work, it is desirable to know how the AT works. The entire unit can be conditionally divided into three parts: mechanics, hydraulics, and electronics. Hydraulics transfer the torque to the mechanical part, which is responsible for shifting gears. The electronics are the brain of the transmission; it switches modes and provides feedback to all car systems and the car owner.

The main goal of the AT is to convert the power and torque of the engine. The key element in the transmission is the torque converter (or, as it is often called, the "bublik"). The bublik creates the coupling based on the load and wheel rotation speed, but does not require active driver intervention. Mechanically, the torque converter consists of a pair of blade units—a centrifugal turbine and a centrifugal pump—with a guiding device-reactor between them.

The mechanical part of the AT also includes a planetary gear set, which changes the gear ratio when shifting gears; a brake band, and a control device. The latter includes a hydraulic block (valve plate), consisting of a large system of channels, oil reservoir, valves, and plungers. Their main task is to control the system, convert the engine load, pedal pressure, and vehicle speed into hydraulic signals. These signals then change the gear ratios.

Knowing how the transmission works, some preventive work can be done independently, avoiding maintenance costs at the service station. But everything depends on the type of breakdown. And here, it gets quite tricky, since there are many AT malfunctions, and their symptoms are very similar.

When is an automatic transmission diagnosis needed?

Logically, if there is a problem with the transmission, the driver will notice it—issues will arise with gear shifting:

  • Vehicle slipping;
  • No reverse, 3rd, or 4th gear;
  • A strong jolt when shifting gears, and the vehicle does not move;
  • Only the 3rd gear engages;
  • Complete failure of the reverse gear;
  • A strong jolt when shifting from "P" mode to "N" mode;
  • Spontaneous gear shifting at high speed.

Malfunctions can also manifest as the vehicle completely stopping or the check engine light coming on. Symptoms of issues include unusual noises, jolts, grinding, burning smells, and noticeable vibrations when starting or driving. In all these cases, a diagnosis is needed.

Faults in the automatic transmission

The complexity of diagnosing the AT lies in the fact that the same symptom can indicate different faults. Sometimes, to determine the malfunction, a visual inspection of the transmission is enough, while other times, it needs to be fully disassembled. In both cases, the symptom remains the same.

Despite the technical complexity of the AT, the most common faults are usually identified. They can be roughly divided into two types:

Faults in the electronics

The most common faults in the electronic part are:

  • Failure of the ECU (electronic control unit).
  • Failure of sensors responsible for controlling the AT and engine systems.
  • Damage to the wiring.
  • Failure of actuators.

One of the advantages of the automatic transmission is that when malfunctions occur in the electronic part, the unit detects it and switches the systems into emergency mode. First, the car owner is notified. Second, the transmission automatically activates special operating systems that help the unit continue to perform its functions. At this point, the transmission only partially works (often, the AT only shifts into 3rd gear), but this is enough for the car to drive to the service station.

Mechanical and hydraulic faults

Since the mechanical and hydraulic parts of the AT are under high load, any component can fail. However, the following units often break down:

  • Gears and shafts.
  • Brake band and clutch disks.
  • Torque converter and its locking clutch.
  • Overrunning clutch of the GDT starter.
  • Hydraulic block.
  • Friction elements, which lead to clogging of oil valves.
  • Oil pump.

Very often, one malfunction leads to another if it is not addressed in time. The principle of AT operation is similar to a snowball, as everything in the transmission is interconnected. For example, if friction disks wear out, they gradually contaminate the oil. The oil, circulating through the hydraulic block, leaves traces of the worn friction disks, which leads to the failure of the plates. This leads to a critical overheating of the AT and its complete failure. Therefore, it is crucial to perform diagnostics and address faults at the early stages.

Causes of AT failures

Surprisingly, there are only a few causes for automatic transmission failures:

  • Natural wear of mechanical components;
  • Use of poor-quality or unsuitable oil;
  • Use of low-quality spare parts;
  • Irregular or untimely maintenance of the transmission;
  • Poor-quality repairs;
  • Extreme or unsuitable driving style according to the manufacturer's recommendations.

The driver will recognize that a malfunction has occurred due to vibrations, strange sounds, burning smells, a decrease in dynamics, an increase in engine RPM, and higher fuel consumption. When symptoms appear, it is essential not to delay and immediately visit a service station for diagnostics, fault detection, and repairs.

How Deep Transmission Diagnostics Are Conducted

In case of obvious failure when symptoms have already appeared, we perform deep diagnostics. It includes a series of works.

Checking the Transmission Fluid

If leaks were noticed on the pan during the initial diagnosis, we check the oil level. This is done through the fill plug on the transmission pan while the engine is running. If the oil spills out, it means there is too much oil, and its quantity needs to be reduced.

Next, we check the oil quality. To do this, it is partially drained or checked with a dipstick. The technician inspects the fluid for impurities, their quantity, color, and consistency. ATF should be yellowish or reddish in color, though darker shades are acceptable depending on its type. If there are milky streaks, foam, burn marks, or a burnt smell, we recommend a full oil change.

Analysis of Deposits in the Transmission Pan

During diagnostics, the pan is removed and the amount of deposits is checked. If there is wear on any mechanical components, products of this process will accumulate in the pan. The type of deposits indicates the location of the failure. If large steel shavings are observed, the failure is likely in the torque converter, supports, shafts, or pump. If there are bronze or copper deposits, it’s likely in the sliding bearings, aluminum fragments or powder in the clutch basket, and jelly-like mass in the friction elements.

Pressure Check

The automatic transmission has special diagnostic plugs where a pressure gauge can be connected to measure the hydraulic line pressure while the transmission is working.

“STOP-START” Test

This test is not always performed but is necessary to detect slippage in the transmission. It involves holding the brake pedal while sharply pressing the gas pedal. It is important to note that this test should only be performed by an experienced specialist, as it places a critical load on the transmission. Even the slightest mistake can lead to serious transmission damage. Therefore, do not risk it, and consult a professional service center for such tests.

Analysis of the Pre-Load Period in the Transmission

This analysis is performed to understand the pressure buildup speed in the hydraulic block circuits. The technician alternates between various modes such as "P -> D", "D -> N -> R", "1 -> 2", "2 -> 3", "3 -> 4", and connects to sport mode, winter mode, or speed limitation for higher gears. If the transmission responds with a characteristic light jolt within 0.5 to 1.2 seconds, it indicates there is no failure. If the period is longer, there may be issues with the hydraulic block.

Deep diagnostics are advisable when performing a gearbox defect check, and its cost depends on the complexity of the work and the extent of the damage.

Computer Diagnostics of the Automatic Transmission

Computer diagnostics are performed using special dealer scanners designed for specific car brands. These scanners have sensor parameter tolerances for the transmission, which help detect failures in real time.

Many service centers work with most car brands, so they often use multi-brand equipment. It is universal but expensive. Therefore, it is not worth purchasing for yourself – diagnostic services at a service center will be much cheaper.

Can I Perform Computer Diagnostics of the Transmission Myself?

Every service center technician understands that most car owners want to save on diagnostics and repairs. However, if you care about the condition of your vehicle, your safety, and the safety of passengers, and if the result is important to you, it is better not to take risks and consult a professional.

Yes, you can use a cable, connect it to the OBD II connector, download software from the internet, and try to decode the error codes yourself. You may also manage to remove the pan and check the condition of the wires under makeshift conditions. However, you must understand that none of your efforts and work can guarantee 100% accuracy in the results. To correctly identify the fault, you need to thoroughly understand how the automatic transmission works, the technical nuances specific to the model installed in your car. There is a fine line, and if a mistake is made, complicated and costly repairs are inevitable.

And if we are talking about saving money, remember that the cost of professional diagnostics will never exceed the cost of repairs if an error was made during a self-conducted diagnosis. It is much more cost-effective to initially consult specialists, accurately identify the fault, and select the correct, rational repair method than to later face the consequences of your savings.

Checking the Transmission While Driving

Automatic transmission testing can also be done while driving. This is a type of diagnostics to identify failure symptoms and further troubleshooting. This type of diagnostics allows for assessing the transmission's operation under various loads.

During diagnostics, the first thing observed is the quality of shifting. If the transmission is cold, small jolts are allowed when lightly pressing the gas pedal. These jolts should not occur if the transmission was previously warmed up.

If you feel bumps, jerks, slipping gears, delays in shifting, clutch slippage, noises, or vibrations during acceleration or braking, this indicates a 100% failure.

Another test can be performed by stopping the car on a hill and releasing the brake pedal. If the car rolls backward, a fault exists. In any case, further detailed diagnostics and subsequent repairs are necessary.

Recommendations and Advice

Transmission diagnostics are necessary in any case: whether buying a car, performing preventive maintenance, or identifying a failure. The more accurate and high-quality the diagnostics, the lower the repair costs will be later. Proper diagnostics by a professional can accurately identify the fault and work on just that. It is also essential to diagnose the transmission in different modes.

In general, transmission diagnostics can be performed independently. However, considering the low cost of the service, self-diagnostics will take a lot of time and effort. Moreover, it will not provide a 100% guarantee of an accurate result, as it will always be done under makeshift conditions and by a person who is not sufficiently competent.

Ideally, diagnostic work, and especially transmission repairs, should be performed by a qualified technician in a designated work facility. Only in such conditions can you expect a complete result from the diagnostics.
