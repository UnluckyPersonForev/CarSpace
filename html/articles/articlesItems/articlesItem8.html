Signs of Automatic Transmission Failure

The automatic transmission is one of the most important components in a car's system. During operation, it is subjected to heavy loads: driving style and duration, overheating, temperature fluctuations, and more. The combination of these loads with the natural wear of transmission parts, untimely maintenance, and failure to follow operating guidelines leads to breakdowns. Every car owner knows that repairing an automatic transmission is a costly process. Therefore, to avoid serious malfunctions or prevent their progression, it is important to recognize the signs of transmission failure in time. Once the component is determined to be faulty, timely intervention and repair are required. In this article, we will explain in detail how to identify the signs of a CVT or automatic transmission failure, what causes these issues, and how to localize the failures.

Key Signs of Transmission Malfunction

Even a beginner car owner can identify if the transmission is malfunctioning. Typically, any malfunction will manifest in unusual behavior of the automatic transmission:

  • Slipping;
  • Vibration;
  • Loss of one of the gears;
  • Failure to switch gears;
  • Unpleasant smell, such as burning;
  • Complete transmission failure, causing the car to stop;
  • Sticking of gear shifts;
  • Malfunction indication on the dashboard.

It is impossible not to notice these signs of malfunction. If one occurs, sooner or later, the driver will definitely feel it. The car immediately begins to behave improperly: from loss of performance to complete failure to move. Recognizing a malfunction in the transmission is easy, but understanding the exact cause and which specific part is faulty is much more complicated.

Common Automatic Transmission Failures

The automatic transmission consists of many parts. It is a complex system that is prone to failure due to improper operation of even the smallest part. However, we will focus on the most common types and causes of automatic transmission failures.

Transmission Fluid

Oil plays a crucial role in the operation of the automatic transmission. Transmission fluid (ATF) forms a film and lubricates moving parts. It is the foundation of the torque converter, which provides engagement, applies pressure to the friction plates, causing gear shifts. It also helps to dissipate the heat generated by the friction plates during operation. Therefore, it is essential to monitor both the quality and quantity of the oil. Unfortunately, not every car owner timely takes the car to specialized service centers, which can lead to breakdowns caused by the transmission fluid.

ATF Condition

Over time, the oil deteriorates and loses its chemical and physical properties. The film it creates becomes thinner, and eventually, it stops forming altogether. Due to friction, without lubrication, metallic shavings form and enter the fluid. As the fluid circulates through the system, the shavings spread to other parts of the unit, causing damage. To prevent this, it is necessary to regularly check the condition of the oil, its color, consistency, presence of shavings, and smell. This can be done manually with a dipstick or by visiting a workshop. It is also important to remember that the automatic transmission fluid should be replaced at regular intervals. The replacement period depends on several factors: the age of the car, mileage, frequency of use, and driving style. The replacement interval is specified in the manufacturer’s technical manual.

Low Oil Level

For the automatic transmission to operate correctly, the oil must be at the appropriate level. It can be checked using a dipstick (with appropriate marks on it). If the oil level is low, and the car continues to be used without refilling, it will lead to a transmission failure. The failure will manifest as slipping, shaking, overheating, or the complete stop of the vehicle. The oil level should be topped up to avoid these issues. This may also indicate a leak.

ATF Leak

A leak is easy to spot—it either appears as an oil puddle under the car or oil in the oil pan. Leaks occur due to gaps forming somewhere in the system: in the connection points of pipes, electronics, or the casing. Usually, the problem is resolved by replacing the faulty part. If a leak is noticed, it should be fixed immediately, or it will cause issues due to a lack of ATF.

Overheating

In most cases, overheating of the automatic transmission is related to insufficient oil, poor oil quality, or improper oil condition. Less frequently, it is due to a malfunction in the cooling system. Overheating can also occur due to excessive vehicle use under certain conditions, which prevents the cooling system from doing its job. In any case, overheated oil will lead to catastrophic consequences, as it interacts with critical transmission components. Overheating negatively affects each of these parts, and hot oil can melt plastic and damage components. To prevent this, it is important to constantly monitor the condition of the radiator and the sensor readings that track the temperature in the transmission system. Temperatures above 100 degrees Celsius are critical and require immediate attention from specialists.

Torque Converter

The torque converter (also known as the "bubbler") is responsible for coupling and smooth gear shifting. It separates the engine from the transmission and then transfers torque, ensuring smooth gear transitions. When the torque converter fails, gear shifts may become sticky, sluggish, or the gears may completely disengage. Fortunately, the torque converter can be repaired, and its restoration will be significantly cheaper than replacing other key transmission components.

Hydraulic Block

The hydraulic plate is the brain of the automatic transmission. It regulates the flow of oil through the entire system, manages gear shifting, and is one of the most important components of the automatic transmission. During vehicle operation, the hydraulic block is subjected to immense stress. As a result, improper maintenance, poor oil quality, and incorrect car usage often lead to its failure. In practice, most hydraulic block failures occur due to the car owner's negligence. For example, driving with cold oil or skimping on maintenance will inevitably lead to problems. Hydraulic block repairs are complex and expensive, so it is often more practical and cost-effective to replace it entirely.

Despite modern diagnostic methods and equipment, identifying a malfunction in this specific component can be difficult. To be absolutely sure of a hydraulic block failure, it is necessary to remove and disassemble the automatic transmission.

Electronic Control Unit

The control unit is responsible for all the electronic aspects of the automatic transmission. A failure in the ECU can lead to various consequences, ranging from errors in transmission operation to complete transmission failure and blockage. The causes of ECU malfunctions may include:

  • Overheating of the oil, causing the melting of plastic and rubber covers, harnesses;
  • Voltage fluctuation leading to electronic failure;
  • Exposure to moisture;
  • Mechanical impact (e.g., after an accident).

Driving a car with a faulty control unit is very dangerous. Any manifestations of failure, such as an error light on the dashboard or other symptoms, should prompt an immediate visit to a specialized service center. The repair of the transmission control unit begins with diagnostics. Error codes are read using a scanner and then decoded. Repair may involve replacing the harnesses, which is easier, or replacing the chips - this is more complex and expensive, as it requires soldering and replacing the circuits. Sometimes it is easier to buy a new ECU and avoid the hassle of repair. In any case, repair work, whether restoration or replacement, should only be performed by experienced specialists in an auto workshop. Saving on this type of repair may lead to negative consequences and more expensive repair services.

Preventing Transmission Failures

There are only two main causes of automatic transmission failures: natural wear and tear and improper vehicle operation (very rarely, manufacturing defects). As practice shows, it is mainly due to improper vehicle operation, untimely maintenance, the use of low-quality oil, and substandard repairs that automatic transmission faults occur. To prevent these faults from developing, it is recommended to:

  • Perform timely maintenance of the transmission with a complete inspection and analysis;
  • Change the oil fully or partially depending on the manufacturer’s recommendations, transmission modification, driving style, frequency, and mileage. Only use high-quality oil recommended by the transmission manufacturer or a good equivalent;
  • When buying a used car or transmission, always change the oil completely;
  • Monitor the radiator condition. If necessary, install additional cooling;
  • Always warm up the vehicle before driving in cold weather;
  • Operate the vehicle correctly, without overloading it. In particular, avoid drifting and rapid acceleration from a standstill;
  • If repairs are needed, only perform them at specialized, licensed service centers with the necessary equipment. Avoid garage mechanics and do not attempt DIY repairs, especially when it involves removing and disassembling the transmission;
  • Perform diagnostics on the automatic transmission every 6-12 months, and replace consumables.

If diagnostics reveal that a part has failed due to natural wear and tear, it should be replaced. It is recommended to install original parts or high-quality equivalents when the original parts are unavailable.

Recommendations for Automatic Transmission Maintenance and Operation

The automatic transmission is quite delicate and vulnerable, but compared to manual transmissions, it is much more thought-out and reliable. However, it still requires adherence to operation rules to avoid failures that could result in costly repairs.

Start driving the car only when the oil is warmed up. At least 2-4 minutes should be spent warming up the vehicle if it has been standing in temperatures between -5 and -15°C. If the temperature is lower, the warm-up should take at least 10 minutes. It is not recommended to start abruptly (full throttle), and you should avoid pressing both the gas and brake pedals simultaneously. For small stops (e.g., at traffic lights) and driving downhill, it’s better to switch the transmission to neutral.

The reverse gear should be shifted only when the vehicle is completely stopped, and when parking or driving in tight spaces, reduce speed using the brake pedal. Always pay attention to the dashboard indicator while driving. If it shows an error, you should urgently consult specialists for diagnostics - this will help prevent a failure in time and prevent it from affecting other transmission parts, significantly reducing repair costs.

Every 10,000-15,000 kilometers, check the level of transmission fluid (either by yourself using the dipstick or by visiting the service center), and every 40,000-60,000 kilometers, partially replace it. The interval for ATF replacement depends on the age of the car, the model of the transmission, and the loads on it. If, when checking the oil, it is very dark, has a burnt smell, or contains metal shavings or other waste materials, it is essential to completely replace the oil and flush the transmission.

It is important to use only the oil recommended by the manufacturer. It doesn’t necessarily have to be a specific brand, as it can sometimes be hard to find (especially for CVTs and other modern transmission types). However, the oil should match the specifications provided by the transmission manufacturer. Using another type of fluid or mixing two different kinds, as well as using cheap alternatives, can lead to accelerated wear of all transmission components and the need for further replacement. Regular maintenance and inspection of the automatic transmission by a specialist will guarantee its long service life without failures. Perform diagnostics on time, monitor its condition, and it will provide you with comfort and safety during your trips without unpleasant surprises.
