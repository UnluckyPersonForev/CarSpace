How often should you change the oil in the automatic transmission?

The eternal dilemma for car owners: when to change the oil in the automatic transmission (AT) – is it necessary, or is it better to leave everything as is? Some people randomly flip through the transmission manual in search of the manufacturer's words “for the entire lifespan,” while others are convinced that the oil in the AT should be changed every 10-15 thousand kilometers. What’s the truth? In this article, we’ll thoroughly examine the issue of replacing the working ATF in an automatic, clearing up all misunderstandings.

Is it necessary to change the oil in the AT, and what functions does it serve?

Motor oil and transmission fluid perform different functions. The latter is especially important for the AT – without it, the transmission simply couldn’t function. Since ATF (Automatic Transmission Fluid) affects several transmission components, it has a specific, unique composition. It is responsible for:

  • The operation of AT components – the oil lubricates them;
  • The system temperature – the fluid dissipates heat from the components that get hot during operation;
  • The functioning of the hydraulics – the oil is essential for the hydraulic system during gear shifting;
  • The functioning of the torque converter (TQ) – ATF transmits the torque from the engine's crankshaft to the planetary gearset in the AT.

    • Such a wide range of responsibilities implies that over time, the oil will change its properties and deplete its useful components. And depending on the vehicle’s operating conditions, this can happen quite quickly.

Why does ATF lose its beneficial properties and age?

Any lubricant will eventually lose its original characteristics and qualities, becoming less effective. Given the stress placed on the oil, this happens regularly. This is caused by several factors:

  • The load on the AT and, as a result, on the oil – this includes driving with cold ATF, long idle times in traffic jams, rapid acceleration (flooring the gas), etc.;
  • Contamination of the fluid via the radiator, which is designed to cool it. Dust and small debris enter through the radiator;
  • The entry of metal shavings into the ATF. The AT contains numerous metal components that rub against each other during operation, creating wear products. These particles enter the oil when the filter is fully clogged and damaged.

Over time, the oil also deteriorates, losing its viscosity and quality, becoming thin. As a result, it fails to perform its functions, directly impacting the condition of nearly all automatic transmission components.

Therefore, it is crucial, even vital, to regularly check the oil's color, quality, viscosity, debris, and smell. If critical deviations are found, the oil must be changed.

When to replace the oil in the AT?

The replacement period is specified by the transmission manufacturer. Of course, this period is somewhat conditional.

For most cars with an automatic transmission, ATF replacement is recommended every 50-60k km with standard vehicle use. In continuously variable transmissions (CVT), ATF should be replaced no less than every 50,000 km. Robotic gearboxes require oil changes every 60-80k km. DSG and Powershift transmissions (though they lack a torque converter) recommend a replacement interval of 50-60k km, after which the oil should be changed.

Driving style and the frequency and type of vehicle use are very important factors in determining the replacement period. If the driving style is aggressive (drifting), the car often drives on dirt roads or off-road, carries heavy loads, tows a trailer, idles frequently in traffic, the oil change intervals mentioned above should be reduced by at least 30%. The same applies when the vehicle is used in extreme heat or cold or is driven at its limits, for instance, during racing or by a car owner who loves high-speed driving for extended periods.

Signs that oil replacement is necessary in the AT

Oil problems (poor quality, loss of lubrication properties, low or excess oil levels) always manifest in transmission malfunctions, and as a result, in vehicle performance. The car often starts jerking, loses driving dynamics, experiences delays when shifting gears, or one of the gears may completely disengage. These symptoms indicate that the problem has worsened, and an oil change is necessary. It’s important to visit a specialized transmission repair service and conduct a diagnostic analysis of the transmission components.

Specific cases for ATF replacement in certain car brands

Most cars within a similar price range with classic ATs require oil replacement at the traditional intervals of 30-60k km, depending on the load on the unit. Toyota cars manufactured before 1999 fall into this category. However, newer Toyota cars require special attention. The thing is, modern vehicles with updated transmission models use a new generation of oil – Toyota Genuine ATF WS or Toyota Genuine ATF type T-IV, which should be replaced no more often than every 90,000 km, even under heavy loads. If the vehicle is used in normal conditions without excessive stress, the car owner only needs to check the oil condition periodically or decide to change it based on certain circumstances, such as overheating the transmission or the car driving with cold oil several times.

How to change the oil in the AT?

Most of the time, oil replacement refers to a partial drain of the used oil and dilution of the remaining fluid with fresh oil. In reality, an unknown amount of dirty oil remains in the torque converter and is mixed with an unknown amount of fresh fluid. The oil is mixed, but even with partial replacement in the AT, it is not in an ideal condition. It’s worth pointing out that partial ATF replacement is profitable for service centers. Since the work isn’t cheap (nor is the oil), it is recommended every 30-60k km, yet it results in a questionable outcome. However, this method remains popular and relevant, as it is still more cost-effective than a full ATF replacement.

Full replacement of the automatic transmission fluid (ATF) is the second method, which is more effective and efficient. It involves completely displacing the old, used oil using a special device and filling it with fresh fluid. At the same time, the filter, pan, or pan gasket is also replaced. The answer to the question "Which is better, a full or partial oil change in the ATF?" is obvious. A full fluid replacement is better and provides 100% efficiency.

However, not all vehicles are allowed to use this method. For example, in some Honda models, the pan is non-removable, which excludes the possibility of fully replacing the fluid. To do this, the pan must be drilled out, and then a new one must be purchased and installed, which is not rational.

Do you need to change the oil in the transmission? Is this true?

20 years ago, to preserve the technical properties of the oil, all automakers, as if by copy, recommended changing the oil after a certain mileage interval. But with the development of technology, manufacturers managed to achieve the effect of adapting the automatic transmission to loads, driving style, and ATF characteristics. And then, leading manufacturers, such as Mercedes, Audi, and BMW, made a breakthrough – they stated that there is no need to replace the oil in the transmission. The label "for the full service life" appeared on the five-speed transmission series 722.6, and a little later, on all ZF-type gearboxes. However, Mercedes and BMW manufacturers, in small print, noted that in harsh driving conditions, oil replacement is necessary. In a short time, this trend was adopted by all automakers, significantly increasing the interval for changing the working fluid. The result can be seen by comparing the technical characteristics of modern transmissions with those of components installed in cars at the end of the 20th century.

Now, all manufacturers, even the leaders, indicate the mileage interval for oil changes. For example, Mercedes recommends changing the oil in the 722.6 transmission (which started the non-replaceable oil trend) every 50,000 km or once every three years. The most advanced versions of the transmission – the seven-speed 722.9 models – require replacement every 125,000 km or every 5 years.

European manufacturers often continue to state that the ATF is filled for the full service life of the vehicle. But there is an important nuance here. Automakers assume that the vehicle will be in service for no more than 5-6 years, after which it will be replaced with a new one. In our realities, cars are often purchased for a much longer period. This implies a sharp need to replace the oil every 5 years, provided the vehicle is used under standard conditions without excessive stress. It is worth noting that each oil change requires replacing the filter.

How to properly change oil in an automatic transmission?

If you still decided to do the job yourself but don’t know how to change the ATF, start with an analysis of it. First, you need to check the oil level in the ATF using the dipstick, which is provided in the equipment.

Next, you need to analyze the oil's quality by draining it into a clean container. If the oil is black, it indicates that it needs to be replaced.

If you smell burning oil or see metal shavings in the fluid, it's time to consult a professional. Shavings and a burning smell indicate that the oil has lost its lubricating properties, which has led to increased friction of metal components and their complete wear. In this case, a diagnosis with a full inspection of each part should be carried out. If neglected, the metal shavings will inevitably get into the electro-hydraulics, leading to severe breakdowns and expensive repairs.

What kind of oil should be used in an automatic transmission?

The ideal option is the original oil that was already filled in the system by the manufacturer or one that is as close as possible in quality and composition to the original. A full equivalent will be the ATF, where the part number listed on the container matches the one installed on the vehicle. However, you can use not only the specific brand recommended by the manufacturer but any other. The key is that it must be a high-quality product.

Other ATFs can also be used, but with great caution. It is better to consult with specialists to avoid damaging the transmission. Before filling the new oil, especially if doing a partial replacement, you should thoroughly familiarize yourself with the properties and technical characteristics of the chosen fluid. Often, the replacement product is of better quality than what was initially filled by the manufacturer. After all, the manufacturer's primary goal is not to provide the highest quality product but one that is optimal in price for production, to achieve the greatest financial gain.

Don't forget that when doing a partial oil change, two types of fluid will inevitably mix, so deviations in quality, composition, and other characteristics of the ATF will affect the operation and overall condition of the transmission. This is why it is very important to use original ATF.

Is it necessary to change the filter in the automatic transmission when changing the oil?

The filter is changed optionally, as needed. If a one-time paper filter is installed, it must be replaced every time the working fluid is changed. However, if a reusable metal filter is used, it requires thorough cleaning of wear products and blockages.

It is also recommended to replace the pan gasket. A new gasket will guarantee the sealing of all connections after disassembling the transmission and replacing the oil.

Conclusion

When using a vehicle with an automatic transmission, it is recommended not only to systematically change the oil but also to monitor its volume and quality yourself. For this, place the dipstick into the designated hole until it stops, check the oil level on the dipstick, and then wipe it with a clean cloth or paper. The color of the ATF will indicate whether it’s time to replace it:

  • Fresh, good oil is clean, transparent, and has a pink or red tint. The color of the fluid will change depending on the vehicle's mileage - this is normal. However, the most important thing is that it remains transparent;
  • If the oil is cloudy, opaque, and you cannot see fabric or paper through it, this indicates that it is already contaminated with dust and other particles and needs to be replaced. Depending on the degree of contamination, the replacement may be either full or partial;
  • If there are metal shavings in the oil, and it smells burnt, you need to go to the service station and diagnose the transmission. Such contamination of the ATF indicates that simply replacing the oil will no longer be sufficient. Transmission repairs may be required, including replacing some worn parts.

Whatever the manufacturers say, we recommend a rational approach to the issue of changing the oil in the ATF and performing it regularly and systematically, without delaying it. Don’t forget that the manufacturer-recommended replacement period is relative. It can vary depending on the transmission load, driving style, operating conditions, and much more.

The work of changing the oil at a service station takes no more than one day (usually just a few hours), and the cost is much cheaper than the repairs that may be needed if the oil is not changed on time. Therefore, periodically check the transmission fluid level and visit the auto repair shop for scheduled replacements.
