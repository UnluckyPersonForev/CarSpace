10 Most Popular Myths About Automatic Transmissions

Despite the wide variety of automatic transmission types, cars with manual transmissions continue to be bought. And not in small numbers. Undoubtedly, a car with a manual transmission has its charm (especially after watching "The Fast and the Furious" :-) ), some people love to feel the gears and control them entirely by themselves. But we know that some car owners are reluctant to switch from manual to automatic simply because they believe in myths about the latter. The internet is full of unfortunate information about automatic transmissions. Who writes it is unknown. Perhaps manual transmission enthusiasts. But we are here to dispel the most popular myths. Let's go!

Cars with automatic transmissions are very fuel-hungry

This statement cannot be called a myth. At least because, to compare fuel consumption, you need to compare two cars with the same technical characteristics and driving style.

First of all, the term "automatic" can refer to a specific type of transmission. This includes CVTs, robots, and classic automatics with a hydraulic torque converter onboard. Each variation has its own technical nuances and, accordingly, its own fuel consumption. For example, a robot is essentially a regular manual with added control modules that engage the clutch.

Secondly, some people consistently drive at 60 km/h, while others race from one traffic light to the next. In this case, the type of transmission doesn't matter. Logically, when driving slowly and smoothly, fuel consumption will be lower.

Maybe the whole point is that manual transmissions are usually found in more economical classes of cars? And in that case, comparing fuel consumption is pointless because you need to compare engine power.

Anyway, there's a popular opinion that automatic transmissions consume 10% more fuel than manual ones. We won't argue; in some situations, that's true. But sometimes, fuel consumption with a manual transmission is identical.

Manual transmissions provide better acceleration

The driver's experience is very important. If a professional is behind the wheel and can shift gears quickly, the myth is quite justified. But such drivers are few and far between on the road.

Fact: Modern automatic transmissions can shift gears in less than a second. Now, let's compare. What are the odds that a driver can depress the clutch, disengage one gear, and engage another in less than one second? Practically zero. Therefore, in 9 out of 10 cases, a car with an automatic transmission will outperform a manual transmission in acceleration.

Automatic transmissions are unreliable

Would a manual transmission be reliable if driven off-road, through mud, drifting, and completely violating the rules of operation? Doubtful. Claims about the unreliability of automatic transmissions are a myth. Because it's all relative. Someone might drive an off-road vehicle through snow or swamp and kill the automatic transmission after just 1 km. Yet, someone else might drive for 200,000 km, and the transmission is still fine. Proper maintenance of the transmission and timely servicing play a huge role. Without this, no transmission will survive.

Automatic transmissions are useless in winter

There is some truth to this. Because in winter, it's harder to get a car with an automatic transmission moving by switching back and forth.

However, most modern automatic transmissions have a winter mode, which helps the car move more easily on icy roads. It's also important to note that in a car with an automatic transmission, the gear is always engaged, unlike a manual transmission, where you often coast in neutral at a stoplight or when shifting gears slowly. Those who know what I'm talking about will understand how important continuous wheel momentum is.

You can't tow a car with an automatic transmission. Only a tow truck

There is a belief that if you tow a car with an automatic transmission, some of its parts will break. This belief is somewhat debatable but partly true.

The transmission needs hydraulic pressure, which is created by the pump in tandem with the engine. Without this, the transmission components cannot function. This means the risk of damaging the clutch during towing is minimal. However, the pump also helps circulate the oil through all the components. If it fails during towing, important components are likely to break.

However, if you can lift the drive axle off the ground, towing will proceed without problems. The transmission won’t even realize it was towed. Also, before towing, check the manufacturer's instructions. Some automakers recommend adding fluid to the automatic transmission before towing and sticking to a certain speed to minimize risks. Note that some automakers say you shouldn’t tow the car, in which case, a tow truck is indeed the best option.

If you decide to tow the car, make sure to switch from park mode to neutral. Otherwise, you might run into problems.

When stopping, you need to switch the automatic transmission to neutral

Still think that when stopping at a traffic light, you must switch to neutral to avoid quickly wearing out the clutch? This is no more than a myth. Perhaps early automatic transmissions required this, but modern units are smart enough. In fact, the automatic transmission doesn’t understand that there is a red light in front of you – it works within a limited set of actions. However, there is a connection between the brake pedal and the transmission. It knows when the brake pedal is pressed and can automatically switch to neutral without additional lever movements.

Fact: the components of the automatic transmission will wear out much faster when switching modes from “D” to “N” than when staying in Drive mode while braking at traffic lights or during traffic jams.

The automatic transmission is not repairable

One of the most ridiculous myths is the belief that the transmission cannot be repaired and that only the entire unit needs to be replaced, which costs a significant amount.

Any automatic transmission, regardless of its year of manufacture and modification, can be repaired. It is possible to repair the torque converter, hydraulic block, replace consumables, seals, oil, and other many removable components. The only “BUT” is for parts that are either impractical or very expensive to repair, making replacement simpler. The same applies to the hydraulic block, for example.

In every city, there are service stations that repair automatic transmissions, where services can be obtained. The question, however, is the competence of the mechanics. Often, mechanics err on the side of caution, understanding that certain repairs are beyond their ability. Therefore, they may insist on the need for a complete replacement of the transmission. But know that this is unreasonable, and it's worth consulting other specialists.

Replacing the automatic transmission is recommended only in a few cases

  • When the repair would cost more than a replacement (for example, after a serious accident);
  • When the transmission is beyond repair.

In all other cases, the automatic transmission can always be restored, and it will cost much less than a complete replacement.

Automatic transmission oil starvation

For some unclear reason, there is a belief that the oil pump operates from the output of the torque converter (TCC). However, in reality, the oil pump drive is connected to the body of the TCC. Therefore, pressure is generated in the system as soon as the engine starts to rotate. Oil circulates in the system in all modes, and starvation is excluded.

Cars with automatic transmissions do not engine brake

This was true only for the first models of automatic transmissions produced last century when the torque converter lacked a locking function. Now, any automatic transmission, even those without an ECU, are produced with a self-locking torque converter. In this mode, the stator of the torque converter and the rotor are connected through a friction clutch. The moment from the engine is transferred to the planetary gears of the automatic transmission, bypassing the hydraulic transmission. This significantly increases the efficiency of the transmission.

It is recommended to manually shift the automatic transmission to lower gears during prolonged pedal releases.

Automatic transmission oil replacement is often necessary and expensive

This is a blatant myth that we do not recommend believing. Changing the oil in the automatic transmission is indeed necessary but is required rarely. Some manufacturers even specify that the oil is meant to last the entire service life, which means 5 years of vehicle operation.

In other cases, oil changes are needed no more frequently than every 30,000 km. Exceptions include aggressive driving, taxi services, or participation in races. If the operating rules are followed, the transmission fluid may not need to be changed until 60,000 or even 80,000 kilometers, depending on the transmission. Read more in the article on how often to change oil in an automatic transmission.

However, it is recommended to periodically check the oil quality. This can be done independently using a dipstick. And don't forget to regularly undergo maintenance with filter replacements, gaskets, and other consumables.

In conclusion

It is unknown who creates myths about automatic transmissions. But what is known is that they should not be believed. If any questions arise regarding the automatic transmission or its maintenance, it's best to ask specialists at a service station. This way, you’ll get truthful answers and eliminate any doubts. Moreover, consultations are usually free.
